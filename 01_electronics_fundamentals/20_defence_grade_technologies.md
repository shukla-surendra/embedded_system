# Defence-Grade Technologies — Electronics, Standards, and Systems

Defence-grade electronics are built and specified to operate where civilian components fail — extreme temperatures, vibration, shock, radiation, electromagnetic interference, and hostile jamming environments. This document covers the technologies, standards, components, and systems used in military electronics.

---

## Part 1: MIL-SPEC — Military Standards

Every component used in defence equipment must meet published military specifications. These define how the part is tested and what it must survive.

### MIL-STD-810 — Environmental Engineering
The primary standard for environmental durability testing. Tests are defined for:

| Test    | Method | What It Covers                                         |
|---------|--------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| Temp low | 502   | Storage and operation down to −62°C                   |
| Temp high| 501   | Up to +85°C operation (or higher for specific apps)   |
| Thermal shock| 503 | Rapid cycling between extremes — tests solder joints |
| Humidity | 507   | 95% RH at elevated temperature — causes corrosion     |
| Vibration| 514   | Sinusoidal and random vibration — aircraft, vehicle   |
| Shock    | 516   | Drop, ballistic shock — up to 40 g, 11 ms            |
| Altitude | 500   | Operation at high altitude / low pressure             |
| Immersion| 512   | Waterproofing under pressure                          |
| Sand/dust| 510   | Fine particles entering enclosures                    |
| Fungus   | 508   | Biological resistance                                 |
| EMI/EMC  | 461   | Electromagnetic interference (separate standard)      |

### MIL-STD-461 — Electromagnetic Compatibility
Defines emission and susceptibility limits for military equipment:
- **CE (Conducted Emissions)**: noise on power lines.
- **RE (Radiated Emissions)**: RF noise radiating from the equipment.
- **CS (Conducted Susceptibility)**: immunity to noise injected on power/signal lines.
- **RS (Radiated Susceptibility)**: immunity to external RF fields.

Military equipment must survive very high field strengths — including the RF fields generated by nearby radar, weapon systems, and deliberate jamming.

### MIL-STD-1275 — Military Vehicle Power
Military vehicle power buses are extremely noisy:
- Nominal 28V DC (not 12V like civilian vehicles).
- Voltage transients from −100V to +100V (cannon fire, EMP).
- Military electronics must survive this — requires transient protection far beyond civilian automotive standards.

### Component Temperature Grades

| Grade       | Temperature Range | Used In                              |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Commercial  | 0°C to +70°C      | Consumer electronics                 |
| Industrial  | −40°C to +85°C    | Automotive, industrial equipment     |
| Military    | −55°C to +125°C   | Aircraft, missiles, ground vehicles  |
| Space       | −55°C to +150°C + radiation | Satellites, spacecraft         |

The same silicon die can exist in all four grades — the difference is the testing, screening, and package. Military-grade parts are individually tested and guaranteed to spec across the full range.

### MIL-PRF-38535 — Military Integrated Circuits
Defines the qualification and quality assurance requirements for military ICs. Parts that pass are listed in the **QPL (Qualified Products List)**. Defence procurement only uses QPL parts for critical systems.

## Part 2: Defence-Grade Communication Technologies

### FHSS and DSSS — Anti-Jam Spread Spectrum

Civilian systems use FHSS and DSSS for interference avoidance. Military systems use them for **anti-jam** (AJ) and **low probability of intercept (LPI)** / **low probability of detection (LPD)**.

**Military FHSS:**
- Hops at up to 30,000 hops/sec (vs 1600 hops/sec for Bluetooth).
- Hopping pattern determined by a **crypto key** — an adversary cannot predict or follow the hops without the key.
- Even if an adversary detects transmissions, they cannot decode them without the key.
- A jammer would need to jam the entire band simultaneously — extremely power-intensive.

**DSSS military (CDMA-based):**
- Spreading code known only to authorised receivers.
- Signal power spread below the noise floor — looks like noise to an adversary.
- Processing gain (dB) = 10 × log(chip rate / data rate) — can be 30–60 dB.
- A 60 dB processing gain means the signal can be 1,000,000× below the noise level and still be recovered.

### SINCGARS — Single Channel Ground and Airborne Radio System
The standard US Army VHF combat net radio since the 1980s.

| Parameter  | Value                                  |
|------------|----------------------------------------|
| Frequency  | 30 – 87.975 MHz (VHF)                 |
| Modulation | FM (voice) + FSK (data)                |
| Hopping    | ECCM mode: 111 hops/sec over 2320 channels |
| Crypto     | NSA Type 1 (COMSEC module)            |
| Range      | ~35 km ground-to-ground               |
| Use        | Army infantry, armoured vehicle comms |

### HAVE QUICK — Anti-Jam UHF Airborne Radio
Military airborne UHF radio with ECCM (frequency hopping):

| Parameter  | Value                            |
|------------|----------------------------------|
| Frequency  | 225 – 400 MHz (military UHF)    |
| Hopping    | HAVE QUICK I: ~7000 frequencies / HAVE QUICK II: faster |
| Sync       | TOD (Time of Day) signal from GPS or broadcast |
| Use        | Fighter aircraft, ATC, SAR coordination |

### Link 16 — Tactical Data Link
The primary NATO tactical data link — connects aircraft, ships, and ground forces in a shared tactical picture.

| Parameter    | Value                                     |
|--------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Frequency    | 960 – 1215 MHz (L-band)                  |
| Modulation   | MSK (Minimum Shift Keying) + TDMA        |
| ECCM         | 51 frequency slots, pseudo-random hopping|
| Crypto       | NSA Type 1                               |
| Data rate    | 31.6 or 57.6 kbps net (per user much less)|
| Range        | Line-of-sight (~500 km air-to-air)       |
| Use          | Track reports, weapons coordination, identification |

Each platform gets specific **time slots** (TDMA) — so hundreds of platforms share the same network without collision.

### MUOS — Mobile User Objective System
Military satellite communication (SATCOM) system replacing legacy UHF SATCOM.

- **Frequency:** UHF (300–400 MHz) + WCDMA waveform (same technology as 3G mobile).
- **Architecture:** 5 geosynchronous satellites + worldwide ground stations.
- **Throughput:** Up to 384 kbps per user (vs ~2.4 kbps for legacy DAMA SATCOM).
- **Use:** Soldiers with handheld terminals, aircraft, submarines (using trailing wire antenna).

### CDL — Common Data Link
Wideband data link for unmanned aircraft (UAV/UAS) and reconnaissance pods.

| Parameter    | Value                                   |
|--------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Frequency    | Ku-band (14–15 GHz) or X-band         |
| Data rate    | Up to 274 Mbps (full-duplex)           |
| Encryption   | Type 1                                  |
| Use          | Streaming full-motion video + sensor data from UAVs to ground station |
| Directional  | Narrow steerable beam — LPI            |

### JTRS — Joint Tactical Radio System
Software-defined radios (SDR) that can implement multiple waveforms in software:
- One hardware radio that can be reprogrammed to operate as SINCGARS, Link 16, SATCOM, WiFi simultaneously.
- Uses the **SCA (Software Communications Architecture)** — a standard API for radio waveform software.
- Key chip: **Xilinx Virtex FPGA** — reconfigurable digital signal processing.

## Part 3: Military GPS — Anti-Jam and Precision

### GPS P(Y) Code — Precision Military Signal
Civilian GPS uses the **C/A code** (Coarse/Acquisition). Military GPS uses:

| Signal   | Access      | Chip Rate   | Processing Gain vs C/A | Use               |
|----------|-------------|-------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| C/A code | Public      | 1.023 Mcps  | Baseline               | Civilian GPS      |
| P(Y) code| Encrypted   | 10.23 Mcps  | +10 dB vs C/A          | Military GPS      |
| M-code   | Encrypted   | 5.115 Mcps  | +20 dB vs P(Y)         | Next-gen military |

**P(Y) code** is 10× wider bandwidth than C/A → 10 dB more processing gain → 10× more resistant to jamming.

**M-code (new):**
- Broadcast from a spot beam — higher EIRP to selected regions only.
- Separate from civilian signals — can be selectively denied to adversaries.
- On-board crypto — no separate crypto receiver needed.
- **MGUE (Military GPS User Equipment):** M-code capable receivers for military platforms.

### CRPA — Controlled Reception Pattern Antenna
An anti-jam antenna used on military GPS receivers:
- Array of 4–7 antenna elements.
- Digital beamforming — steers nulls (blind spots) toward jamming sources.
- Can suppress 3–6 simultaneous jammers by more than 40 dB.
- Used on aircraft, vehicles, precision-guided munitions.

```
CRPA operation:
  Jammer → signals arrive from one direction
  Beamformer places a null in jammer direction
  GPS satellites (distributed across sky) remain in the main beam
  Jammer suppressed by 40+ dB → receiver works normally
```

### INS/GPS Integration
Military platforms integrate GPS with an **INS (Inertial Navigation System)**:
- **INS** (accelerometers + gyroscopes) — provides position continuously but drifts over time.
- **GPS** — accurate position but can be jammed or spoofed.
- **Kalman filter** tightly couples them — INS fills GPS gaps, GPS corrects INS drift.
- During GPS jamming, INS maintains navigation for minutes to hours depending on grade.

**INS grades:**

| Grade            | Drift Rate          | Technology                  | Application            |
|------------------|---------------------|-----------------------------|------------------------|
| Automotive       | ~10 m/min           | MEMS IMU                    | Car navigation         |
| Tactical         | 1 – 10 m/min        | Higher-quality MEMS / RLG   | Drones, missiles       |
| Navigation       | 0.1 – 1 m/min       | Ring Laser Gyro (RLG)       | Aircraft, submarines   |
| Strategic        | < 0.1 m/min         | Electrostatic gyroscope     | ICBMs, submarines      |

**Ring Laser Gyro (RLG):** Two laser beams travel in opposite directions around a triangular path. When the platform rotates, the Sagnac effect causes one beam to take slightly longer — the phase difference = rotation rate. No moving parts — extremely accurate and reliable.

## Part 4: Electronic Warfare (EW)

Electronic warfare is the use of the electromagnetic spectrum as a weapon and a shield.

### The Three Pillars of EW

```
                    ELECTRONIC WARFARE
                           │
          ┌────────────────┼────────────────┐
          │                │                │
   Electronic Attack  Electronic      Electronic
       (EA)           Protection          Support
                        (EP)               (ES)
   (jamming, spoofing) (ECCM, filtering) (SIGINT, direction finding)
```

### Electronic Attack (EA)

**Jamming** — transmitting RF energy to mask or confuse enemy receivers.

| Type              | How It Works                                         | Best Against              |
|-------------------|------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Spot jamming      | High-power on exact enemy frequency                 | Fixed-frequency comms     |
| Sweep jamming     | Sweep across frequency band                         | Multiple frequencies      |
| Barrage jamming   | Wideband noise across entire band simultaneously    | FHSS (brute force)        |
| Deceptive jamming | Generates false targets / false positions           | Radar, GPS                |
| Escort jamming    | Jammer aircraft flies with strike package           | Enemy SAM radars          |
| Stand-off jamming | Jammer stays outside threat area, beams power in   | Area suppression          |

**Burn-through range:** As a jammer approaches the radar it is jamming, the jamming-to-signal ratio reverses — the target return eventually overpowers the jamming. This is the burn-through range. Jammers must stay beyond this range.

```
Jamming equation:
  J/S = (Pj × Gj × σ) / (Pt × Gt × λ² × F)

  J/S must be > 1 (jammer must be stronger than target return)
  Pj = jammer power
  Pt = radar transmit power
  σ  = radar cross section of target
```

**DRFM — Digital Radio Frequency Memory:**
The modern standard for electronic attack:
- Captures incoming radar pulse digitally.
- Stores the exact waveform.
- Retransmits it with modifications (delay, Doppler shift, changed power).
- Creates **false targets** that appear real on the radar — operator sees 10 aircraft instead of 1.
- Because the jamming replicates the exact radar waveform, it defeats simple blanking filters.

**GPS Spoofing:**
- Transmits fake GPS signals slightly stronger than real satellites.
- Receiver locks onto fake signals — reports false position.
- Used to misdirect drones and ships.
- Countermeasure: receiver authentication (M-code, Galileo OSNMA — Open Service Navigation Message Authentication).

### Electronic Protection (EP)

**ECCM (Electronic Counter-CounterMeasures)** — making your own systems resistant to jamming:

| Technique            | How It Works                                             |
|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| FHSS / DSSS          | Spread spectrum — force jammer to cover entire band     |
| Pulse compression (LPI radar) | Wideband chirp — hard to jam, hard to detect  |
| Ultra-low sidelobes  | Antenna design — jammer can only enter via main beam    |
| CRPA antenna         | Null steering toward jammer direction                   |
| Space-time adaptive processing (STAP) | Suppress jammers using array processing   |
| Frequency agility    | Radar changes frequency pulse to pulse                  |
| High PRF             | Difficult to predict and jam specific pulses            |
| Waveform diversity   | Change waveform so DRFM capture is harder               |
| GPS M-code           | Higher processing gain, spot beam power advantage       |

### Electronic Support (ES) / SIGINT

**Direction Finding (DF):**
- Multiple antenna elements receive the same signal at slightly different times.
- Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) across the array → bearing to emitter.
- Multiple DF stations triangulate the emitter's position.
- Used to locate enemy radars, radios, and missile guidance systems.

**ELINT (Electronic Intelligence):**
- Passively intercept and analyse radar signals.
- Characterise radar type, scan rate, frequency agility, PRF.
- Build a library of emitter fingerprints — identify the system type from its signal.
- Used to pre-programme anti-radiation missiles to home on specific radar types.

**COMINT (Communications Intelligence):**
- Intercept voice and data communications.
- Even if encrypted — traffic analysis reveals patterns (who communicates with whom, how often).

## Part 5: Radar — Military-Specific Technologies

### AESA — Active Electronically Scanned Array
Modern fighter aircraft radar — covered in radar doc but expanded here:

**Architecture:**
- Thousands of **T/R modules** (Transmit/Receive modules), each containing:
  - A GaN power amplifier (transmit).
  - A low-noise amplifier (receive).
  - A phase shifter (beam steering).
  - An attenuator (amplitude weighting for sidelobe control).
- Each module is individually controlled by the radar computer.

**Military advantages over PESA (Passive ESA) and mechanical scan:**
- **Instantaneous beam agility** — point anywhere in microseconds.
- **Multiple simultaneous beams** — track targets while scanning.
- **LPI waveform** — vary frequency, power, PRF randomly per pulse → impossible to fingerprint.
- **Graceful degradation** — individual T/R module failures don't kill the radar.
- **Electronic attack** — can radiate as a directed energy jammer toward threats.
- **ISAR (Inverse SAR)** — image targets at long range.

**Examples:** F-22 AN/APG-77, F-35 AN/APG-81, F-16 AN/APG-83 (SABR upgrade), Eurofighter Captor-E.

### ISAR — Inverse Synthetic Aperture Radar
Used to produce high-resolution radar images of ships and aircraft:
- **SAR** moves the radar platform — the target is stationary.
- **ISAR** — the target moves (rotates) — the radar can be stationary.
- Target's rotation creates a synthetic aperture in angle domain.
- Result: 2D image of the target showing its shape — can identify ship type/class.

### Millimetre Wave Radar (mmWave) in Weapons
- **Brimstone/JAGM/Spike:** Anti-tank missiles use 94 GHz radar seekers.
  - Very high resolution at short range.
  - Terminal guidance — locks onto a specific tank among many vehicles.
  - Millimetre wave penetrates smoke and dust — works when IR seekers fail.
- **Phalanx / CIWS:** 20 mm Gatling gun with Ka-band radar for anti-missile defence — tracks rounds in flight and corrects aim.

### LPI Radar
Low Probability of Intercept radar — used by modern fighters and ships to avoid triggering an enemy radar warning receiver (RWR):

| LPI Technique       | How It Works                                              |
|---------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
| FMCW waveform       | Low peak power, high average power — hard to detect      |
| Wide bandwidth chirp| Power spread across wide band — below noise in any narrow band |
| Low sidelobe antenna| Power directed only at target — minimal energy elsewhere  |
| Frequency agility   | Each pulse on a different frequency                       |
| Power management    | Only transmit minimum power needed for each target range  |

**Picasso / PILOT:** Specific LPI radars on Gripen and Rafale fighters — a fighter can use radar without lighting up the enemy's RWR.

## Part 6: IFF — Identification Friend or Foe

IFF is a system that allows military platforms to identify each other without visual contact.

### How IFF Works (Mark XII / Mode 4 / Mode 5)

```
Interrogator (fighter / ground radar)
     │
  Transmits encrypted challenge pulse (1030 MHz)
     │
     ▼
Target aircraft (transponder)
     │
  Receives challenge
  Decrypts with shared crypto key
  Transmits correct encrypted reply (1090 MHz)
     │
     ▼
Interrogator
     │
  Receives reply
  Decrypts — correct reply = FRIEND
  No reply or wrong reply = UNKNOWN / HOSTILE
```

**Mode 4:** Cold War standard — 3 DES crypto.
**Mode 5:** Current NATO standard — AES crypto, GPS position included in reply, much harder to spoof.

**ADS-B (civilian equivalent):** Commercial aircraft broadcast their position, altitude, and identity on 1090 MHz — no crypto, anyone can receive it. This is why civilian ADS-B is separate from military IFF.

## Part 7: Directed Energy Weapons (DEW)

### High-Power Microwave (HPM)
Generates an intense microwave pulse to destroy electronics in enemy systems.

| System     | Type    | Effect                                            |
|------------|---------|---------------------------------------------------|
| CHAMP (USAF)| Cruise missile with HPM payload | Burns out electronics in buildings below |
| THOR       | Counter-drone HPM system        | Fries drone electronics at range        |
| E-bomb     | Warhead that generates EMP on detonation | Disables electronics in area   |

**Effect on electronics:**
- Microwave pulse induces large currents in PCB traces and cable interconnects.
- Destroys gate oxides in MOSFETs and CMOS logic.
- Burns out RF front-end LNAs — most vulnerable component.
- Even if main CPU survives, communications are killed.

**Hardening against HPM:**
- Metal enclosures (Faraday cage) — attenuates field inside.
- Transient voltage suppressors (TVS diodes) on all cable entry points.
- Waveguide-below-cutoff filters on ventilation holes.
- Fibre optic interconnects — immune to conducted HPM.
- Radiation-hardened (RadHard) ICs — slower but survive higher fields.

### High-Energy Laser (HEL)
Focused laser beam to heat and destroy targets:
- **HELIOS (Lockheed):** 60 kW laser on US Navy destroyers — engages drones and small boats.
- **SHORAD:** High-Energy Laser Short-Range Air Defence — counter-drone, counter-rocket.
- **Iron Beam (Israel):** Laser layer of Iron Dome — engages mortar rounds and small UAVs.

**Limitations:** Atmospheric absorption (dust, humidity, clouds), dwell time required on target, thermal blooming in humid air. Beam spread with range limits max engagement distance.

## Part 8: Radiation-Hardened Electronics

In space and nuclear environments, ionising radiation damages and destroys normal electronics.

### Radiation Effects on Electronics

| Effect         | Cause                              | Result                                         |
|----------------|------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| SEU (Single Event Upset) | High-energy particle flips a single bit in memory | Data corruption — "bit flip" |
| SEL (Single Event Latchup) | Particle triggers parasitic thyristor in CMOS | Device draws max current, overheats — destructive |
| TID (Total Ionising Dose) | Accumulated radiation charge in gate oxide | Threshold voltage shift, gain loss, eventual failure |
| Displacement damage | Neutrons knock atoms out of crystal lattice | Carrier lifetime reduction, BJT gain drops |

### Radiation Hardening Techniques

**Process hardening (RHBP — Rad-Hard by Process):**
- SOI (Silicon on Insulator) — thin silicon layer on insulating substrate — charge from radiation cannot spread and cause latchup.
- Thicker gate oxides (reduces charge trapping).
- Special implants to eliminate parasitic structures.

**Design hardening (RHBD — Rad-Hard by Design):**
- **TMR (Triple Modular Redundancy):** 3 copies of every logic block vote on the output — one SEU flip is outvoted.
- Guard rings: ring of P+ or N+ diffusion around transistors — collects stray charge before it causes latchup.
- **ECC (Error Correcting Code) memory:** Can detect and correct single-bit flips in RAM.

**Example RadHard parts:**
| Part          | Rad-Hard Equivalent  | TID Rating    | Use                        |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------|----------------------------|
| Virtex-5 FPGA | Virtex-5QV           | 1 Mrad        | Satellite payload processing|
| LEON3 CPU     | UT699 (Aeroflex)     | 300 krad      | ESA spacecraft             |
| Flash memory  | AT60142 (Microsemi)  | 100 krad      | Satellite storage          |
| LDO regulator | TPS7H1201-SP (TI)    | 300 krad      | Space power                |

## Part 9: Military Sensors

### FLIR — Forward Looking Infrared
Thermal imaging camera for detection and targeting in darkness, smoke, and poor weather.

**How it works:**
- Focal Plane Array (FPA) of microbolometers or photon detectors.
- Each pixel measures IR radiation → temperature → image.

| Detector Type    | Band         | Cooling       | Sensitivity | Use                    |
|------------------|--------------|---------------|-------------|------------------------|
| Microbolometer   | LWIR (8–14 μm) | Uncooled     | Lower       | Handheld cameras, UAV  |
| InSb             | MWIR (3–5 μm) | Cooled (77K) | Higher      | Missile seekers, targeting pods |
| HgCdTe (MCT)     | MWIR + LWIR  | Cooled (77K) | Highest     | Strategic ISR, space   |
| QWIP             | LWIR         | Cooled        | High        | Large-format arrays    |

**Cooled vs uncooled:**
Cooled detectors use a **Stirling cycle cryocooler** to reach 77K (−196°C). At room temperature, the detector itself glows in IR — you need to cool it below the scene temperature to see the scene clearly. Cooling improves sensitivity (NETD) by 10–100×. Disadvantage: cooling mechanism adds size, weight, power, and wear.

### SAL — Semi-Active Laser Homing
Precision guided munitions lock onto a **laser spot** painted on the target by a designator.

**How it works:**
- Designator (aircraft pod or ground soldier) illuminates target with pulsed 1064 nm Nd:YAG laser.
- Laser pulse is coded (specific PRF) — weapon knows which code to home on (prevents other designators confusing it).
- Weapon seeker (quadrant detector or PSD array) detects reflected laser energy.
- Guidance computer steers toward laser spot.

**Components:**
- **Quadrant detector:** 4-element silicon or InGaAs photodiode — off-centre spot creates unequal signals → steer toward equal signal (centre).
- **Bandpass optical filter:** Passes 1064 nm, rejects sunlight — ratio of ~10,000:1 rejection needed.
- **Seeker gimbal:** 2-axis gyro-stabilised gimbal keeps seeker pointed at target regardless of weapon spin/manoeuvre.

### APKWS / DAGR — Laser Guidance Kits for Rockets
Adds SAL seeker to unguided 70 mm Hydra rockets — turns them into precision weapons for counter-drone, counter-insurgency use.

### Millimetre Wave Seeker
Some anti-tank and anti-ship missiles use 94 GHz active radar seekers for all-weather terminal homing — works when laser / IR is obscured.

## Part 10: Counter-Drone Technologies (C-UAS)

Counter-UAS is one of the fastest-growing areas of defence electronics — directly relevant to understanding drone vulnerabilities.

### Detection Methods

| Method           | Technology                | Advantages                          | Limitations                        |
|------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Radar            | FMCW / Doppler (24/77 GHz) | All-weather, long range, passive   | Small drones have tiny RCS         |
| RF detection     | Spectrum monitor 2.4/5.8 GHz | Detects control link              | Autonomous drones don't transmit   |
| Acoustic         | Microphone array           | Cheap, passive                     | Short range, wind noise            |
| FLIR/optical     | Thermal + EO camera         | Works day/night                    | Range limited, weather sensitive   |
| ADS-B tracking   | 1090 MHz receiver          | Commercial drones must broadcast   | Uncooperative drones don't        |

### Defeat Methods

| Method           | How It Works                                                        |
|------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| RF jamming       | Jam 2.4 GHz + 5.8 GHz + GPS → drone loses control, hovers or lands |
| GPS spoofing     | Feed drone false GPS position → guides it away or into ground      |
| Directed Energy  | HPM (THOR) or laser (Iron Beam) — physically destroys              |
| Kinetic interceptor | Nets, projectiles, hit-to-kill missiles (Coyote, SHORAD)       |
| Cyber / signal   | Reverse-engineer control protocol, inject commands                  |
| Eagles/net-guns  | Low-tech but effective for close-range                              |

### Drone Vulnerabilities Targeted by C-UAS

Understanding these helps you build more robust civilian drones:

| Vulnerability     | Exploit                    | Countermeasure                         |
|-------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| 2.4 GHz RC link   | Jamming whole band         | FHSS, redundant frequencies            |
| GPS dependency    | GPS jamming / spoofing     | Optical flow, barometric, INS fallback |
| Unencrypted MAVLink | Protocol injection       | MAVLink 2 signing, encrypted channels  |
| DJI OcuSync       | Known frequencies jammed   | Frequency diversity, 5.8 GHz option   |
| Constant RF emission | RF detection           | Minimise unnecessary transmissions     |

| Function            | Algorithm    | Key Size    | Notes                              |
|---------------------|--------------|-------------|-------------------------------------|

## Part 12: Key Defence-Grade Components

### GaN HEMT — The Transistor of Military RF

Gallium Nitride High Electron Mobility Transistors dominate military RF power applications:

| Parameter        | GaN HEMT        | GaAs pHEMT    | Silicon LDMOS  |
|------------------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| Breakdown voltage| 80–200V         | 10–20V        | 65–130V        |
| Power density    | 5–10 W/mm       | 0.5–1 W/mm    | 0.5 W/mm       |
| Frequency        | DC – 100+ GHz   | DC – 100 GHz  | DC – 4 GHz     |
| Efficiency (PAE) | 50–70%          | 40–60%        | 50–70%         |
| Ruggedness       | Excellent        | Moderate      | Good           |
| Use              | AESA T/R modules, EW, radar PA | LNA, receive | L-band radar, cellular |

**Why GaN for military:**
- High breakdown voltage — survives reflected power from antenna in jamming environments.
- High power density — compact T/R modules in AESA arrays.
- High efficiency — reduces cooling requirements in aircraft pods.
- High frequency — works at X/Ku/Ka-band for modern radar and EW.

### RadHard Voltage Regulators and FPGAs
- **LT3032 (Analog Devices):** Radiation-tolerant dual LDO — space applications.
- **Xilinx Virtex-5QV FPGA:** 1 Mrad TID, SEU immune — used in satellite payload.
- **UT699 LEON3FT:** Radiation-hardened SPARC CPU — used in multiple ESA/NASA spacecraft.
- **Aeroflex UT54ACS logic:** RadHard standard logic family — replaces 74HC series in space/nuclear.

### Crystal Oscillators — Military Grade

| Grade     | Aging               | Temperature Stability | Use                          |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------------|------------------------------|
| Standard XTAL | 5 ppm/year     | ±50 ppm over −20 to +70°C | Consumer electronics    |
| TCXO      | 1 ppm/year          | ±0.5 ppm over −40 to +85°C | GPS receivers, radio   |
| OCXO      | 0.01 ppm/year       | ±0.01 ppm over −40 to +85°C | Military radios, instrumentation |
| Atomic (Rb)| 0.0001 ppm/year   | ±0.0001 ppm           | GPS satellites, SINCGARS timing |
| Atomic (Cs)| 0.00001 ppm/year  | ±0.000001 ppm         | GPS master clocks, standards labs |

**OCXO (Oven Controlled Crystal Oscillator):** Crystal kept in a temperature-controlled oven at constant temperature — achieves extremely stable frequency. Military radios use OCXO to maintain frequency synchronisation for TDMA networks (Link 16, SINCGARS ECCM).

### MIL-STD-1553 — Avionics Data Bus
Found in virtually every military aircraft since the 1970s:
- **1 Mbps** serial bus (slow by modern standards, but extremely reliable and well-understood).
- **Differential twisted pair** — very noise-immune.
- **Command/response** — one Bus Controller, up to 31 Remote Terminals.
- Each transaction is commanded by the BC — no collisions possible.
- Used to connect: flight computer ↔ weapons ↔ navigation ↔ sensors.
- Still used on F-35, alongside modern alternatives like **ARINC 664 (AFDX)** Ethernet for high-bandwidth data.
